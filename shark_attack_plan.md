# Shark Attack Visualisation Plan
**FIT3179 Data Visualisation 2 — Semester 1, 2026**

## Data Sources
- **Primary (AU):** `data/base_data/locdat2.txt` — 1,203 Australian shark incidents, 1791–2022. Fields: lat/long, state, location, species, shark length, activity, injury severity, body part, gender, age, time of day, provoked/unprovoked.
- **Secondary (CA):** `data/base_data/SharkIncidents_1950_2022_220302.xlsx` — 220 California white shark incidents. Fields: lat/long, county, activity, injury severity, species, date.

---

## Section 1: Setting the Scene — "Australia and Sharks"
*Purpose: Orient the viewer. Establish scale, geography, and history before diving into analysis. Should feel like an intro that makes the audience go "wow, I didn't realise it was that many."*

---

### 1.1 — Dot Map of Every Incident
**Idiom:** Point/dot map
**Fields:** `Latitude`, `Longitude`, `Victim.injury`, `Incident.year`
**Story:** Every shark attack in Australia since 1791, plotted on the map. Points coloured by outcome (fatal = red, injured = orange, uninjured = grey). Immediately shows the coastal concentration, the NSW/QLD dominance, and some surprising inland/river incidents (Brisbane River, Parramatta River).
**Interactive:** Tooltip showing year, location, species, outcome. Filter by decade using a slider.
**Custom element:** Filter or highlight to show only fatals — the map thins dramatically and the story shifts to specific hotspot beaches.

---

### 1.2 — Choropleth: Incidents vs Fatal Rate by State
**Idiom:** Two choropleths side by side (or toggled)
**Fields:** `State`, `Victim.injury` (derived: count per state, fatal rate per state)
**Story:** NSW has the most attacks (438) but QLD and SA have the highest fatal rates (~27%). WA is often feared but is actually mid-range on fatality. The volume story and the danger story are completely different maps.
**Derived data:** Aggregate incidents per state + calculate fatal % per state — two separate metrics driving two colour scales.
**Custom element:** A toggle or annotation layer that flips between "number of attacks" and "% fatal" — the choropleth recolours and the viewer sees how differently states rank depending on what you measure.

---

### 1.3 — Timeline: Attacks Per Decade (1791–2022)
**Idiom:** Annotated area/line chart
**Fields:** `Incident.year` (derived: grouped by decade)
**Story:** Attacks were low and flat until the 1910s, grew steadily, dipped in the 1940s (WW2 — fewer people at beaches), and exploded from 2000 onwards. The 2010s had 231 incidents vs 36 in the 1970s.
**Key annotation markers:** Introduction of shark nets (1937 NSW), Newcastle earthquake (1989 spike?), drum line controversy (2014 WA), COVID-19 (2020 drop and then 2021 spike as people returned to beaches).
**Custom element:** Derived field — normalise by Australian population per decade using ABS census data to show whether the *rate* actually increased or just raw numbers. This is a genuinely custom derived insight the tutor will notice.

## Section 2: Which Shark Should You Actually Fear?
*Purpose: Species breakdown. Subvert the Jaws narrative — the most common shark is harmless, the deadliest isn't the most common. Build towards a nuanced picture of real risk.*

---

### 2.1 — Species Bubble Chart: Frequency vs Lethality vs Size
**Idiom:** Custom bubble/scatter plot
**Fields:** `Shark.common.name`, `Victim.injury`, `Shark.length.m` (all derived: count per species, fatal rate per species, median length per species)
**Story:** X axis = number of attacks, Y axis = fatality rate, bubble size = median shark length. Wobbegong sits bottom-left (200 attacks, 0% fatal, small). Tiger shark is top-right (227 attacks, 37.9% fatal, large). White shark is large bubble, mid-right (360 attacks, 25.3% fatal). Bull shark punches above its size. This chart cannot be easily generated by AI — it requires deriving 3 metrics per species.
**Custom element:** Colour the bubbles by a 4th dimension — whether the shark is a "provoked" attacker or "unprovoked" hunter (derived from the data).

---

### 2.2 — The Wobbegong Exception
**Idiom:** Small stacked bar or highlight callout
**Fields:** `Shark.common.name`, `Victim.injury`, `Victim.activity`
**Story:** 200 wobbegong incidents, zero fatalities — ever. They bite when stepped on, sat on, or handled. Pure self-defence. Contrast with white/tiger/bull which actively hunt. A single callout chart or annotated bar that draws attention to this outlier.
**Custom element:** Show the activity breakdown for wobbegongs specifically — most bites happen while diving or standing in water, reinforcing the "you disturbed it" narrative.

---

### 2.3 — Shark Length vs Injury Severity
**Idiom:** Beeswarm or strip plot
**Fields:** `Shark.length.m`, `Injury.severity`, `Shark.common.name`
**Story:** Sharks under 1.5m almost never cause major lacerations. The threshold where sharks become genuinely dangerous sits around 2m+. White sharks cluster at 3–6m and dominate the major laceration category.
**Derived data:** Filter out rows missing shark length (~50% have it), bin by 0.5m intervals, show severity distribution within each bin.
**Custom element:** Overlay the average human height (1.7m) as a reference line — when the shark is bigger than you, the risk profile changes dramatically.

## Section 3: Where, When, and What Were You Doing?
*Purpose: Risk profiling. Help the audience understand their own personal risk based on behaviour. The most actionable section.*

---

### 3.1 — Activity Risk Matrix: Volume vs Fatality Rate
**Idiom:** Custom diverging/lollipop chart or scatter
**Fields:** `Victim.activity`, `Victim.injury` (derived: count + fatal rate per activity)
**Story:** Swimming has 451 incidents AND a 34.6% fatality rate — most dangerous by both measures. Boarding has 281 incidents but only 9.6% fatal. Spearfishing has 118 incidents and 9.3% fatal — you're provoking the shark so it bites and retreats. Fishing: 37 incidents, 0% fatal.
**Derived data:** Compute fatal rate per activity. Create a "risk score" = normalise (fatal_rate × sqrt(count)) to weight both lethality and frequency.
**Custom element:** A 2-axis lollipop where one arm shows count and the other shows fatal %, making the divergence between volume and danger visually immediate.

---

### 3.2 — Seasonal Heatmap by State
**Idiom:** Heatmap (month × state grid)
**Fields:** `Incident.month`, `State` (derived: count per month-state combination)
**Story:** NSW attacks peak sharply in January (summer holidays). QLD stays elevated longer into autumn due to warmer water year-round. WA has a flatter distribution. SA and VIC are low volume but spike in summer. The pattern shows how geography shapes seasonality.
**Derived data:** Pivot table of month × state counts, normalised within each state so you can compare patterns not just volumes.
**Custom element:** A 7×12 grid heatmap — states as rows, months as columns, colour intensity = normalised attack frequency. Clean, dense, informative.

---

### 3.3 — Time of Day Radial Chart
**Idiom:** Radial bar / clock chart
**Fields:** `Time.of.incident` (derived: extract hour, count per hour)
**Story:** Peak attack hour is 4pm (55 incidents), with a secondary morning peak at 11am. Near-zero activity between midnight and 5am. The 4pm peak aligns with afternoon beach crowds + low sun angle reducing underwater visibility.
**Derived data:** Parse `Time.of.incident` (currently stored as e.g. "1600") → extract hour → aggregate.
**Custom element:** A circular clock-face bar chart where bar height = number of attacks. Fatal incidents overlaid as a separate colour ring inside the bars.

---

### 3.4 — Body Part Heatmap (Human Silhouette)
**Idiom:** Custom annotated body diagram
**Fields:** `Injury.location` (derived: count per body region)
**Story:** Legs are by far the most targeted (213), followed by feet (116) and thighs (90). Torso attacks (79) are rarer but far more often fatal. Arms mid-range. This distribution reflects how humans stand in water — lower limbs are most exposed and most visible to sharks below.
**Derived data:** Map the injury location strings to body regions on a SVG human outline. Colour regions by attack frequency.
**Custom element:** A clickable or layered SVG silhouette — click a body part to see which shark species caused those injuries. Full custom build, high visual impact, impossible to AI-generate.

---

## Section 4: Who Gets Attacked?
*Purpose: Victim profiling. Demographics of shark attack victims. Some counterintuitive findings here.*

---

### 4.1 — Gender: Volume vs Fatality
**Idiom:** Isotype / unit chart
**Fields:** `Victim.gender`, `Victim.injury` (derived: counts + fatal rates)
**Story:** Males account for 1,060 of 1,177 incidents — 90% of all attacks. But their fatality rate (21.9%) is actually higher than females (13.7%). Men are in the water more, taking more risks, in riskier activities. The gender gap in fatality rate is smaller than the gap in exposure.
**Custom element:** Two rows of person icons (male/female), each icon = 10 incidents, coloured red for fatal, orange for injured, grey for uninjured. Isotype charts communicate proportions without requiring statistical literacy.

---

### 4.2 — Age Distribution of Victims
**Idiom:** Histogram with kernel density overlay
**Fields:** `Victim.age` (derived: clean numeric, remove bad entries)
**Story:** Peak attack age is 17, with most victims between 10–35. Makes sense — young people surf and swim more. Fatal attacks skew slightly older (adults more likely to be diving/swimming in deeper water).
**Derived data:** Clean the age field (has some corrupt entries like "32" appearing as gender), bin into 5-year groups, overlay a smoothed density curve. Separate density for fatal vs non-fatal.
**Custom element:** Split histogram — fatal incidents on top half, non-fatal on bottom half, mirrored on same axis. Immediately shows the age shift between the two groups.

---

### 4.3 — Provoked vs Unprovoked Deep Dive
**Idiom:** Stacked proportional bar + annotation
**Fields:** `Provoked.unprovoked`, `Victim.injury`, `Victim.activity`
**Story:** Provoked attacks (398 total) have a 13.3% fatality rate. Unprovoked (794) have 24.8%. Counterintuitive — provoking a shark is safer because the shark bites defensively and retreats rather than pursuing. The activity breakdown shows provoked attacks dominate spearfishing and diving; unprovoked dominate swimming.
**Derived data:** Cross-tabulate provocation × severity, compute proportions within each group.
**Custom element:** Paired proportional bars with an annotation callout explaining the mechanism — defensive bite vs predatory attack.

---

## Section 5: Australia vs California
*Purpose: International comparison using the California dataset as second source. Broadens the story, satisfies two-source requirement, gives global context.*

---

### 5.1 — Side-by-Side Activity Comparison
**Idiom:** Grouped or mirrored bar chart
**Fields (AU):** `Victim.activity` (aggregated) | **Fields (CA):** `Mode` (aggregated)
**Story:** In California, surfing/boarding dominates (it's a surf culture state). In Australia, swimming dominates. This reflects the different beach cultures — Californians surf, Australians swim. The risk profile is completely different despite both being major white shark zones.
**Derived data:** Standardise activity categories across both datasets (they use different naming), compute % of total per activity per region.

---

### 5.2 — Fatal Rate Comparison by Decade
**Idiom:** Dual line chart (AU vs CA)
**Fields:** `Incident.year` + `Victim.injury` (AU) | `Date` + `Injury` (CA)
**Story:** California's fatality rate has declined significantly since the 1950s (better medical response, awareness). Australia's rate has also declined but remains higher. The trajectories and the gap between them tells a story about differences in response, shark behaviour, and geography.
**Derived data:** Bin by decade, compute fatal rate per decade per region, plot as two lines.

---

### 5.3 — Map: AU Hotspots vs CA Hotspots
**Idiom:** Two dot maps side by side (or a split panel)
**Fields (AU):** `Latitude`, `Longitude` | **Fields (CA):** `Latitude`, `Longitude`
**Story:** Australia's incidents are spread along a massive coastline. California's are concentrated in a tight band from San Diego to Mendocino — the "Red Triangle." Visually comparing the two coastlines at the same scale makes Australia's geographic spread obvious.
**Custom element:** Scale both maps to the same zoom level for honest comparison. Size dots by injury severity.

### Chart priority for HD marks:
The following require derived data or custom idioms and are most likely to differentiate your submission:
1. Species bubble chart (3 derived metrics per species)
2. Body silhouette heatmap (custom SVG)
3. Radial time-of-day clock chart
4. Normalised decade trend (population-adjusted)
5. Activity risk matrix (derived risk score)

### Suggested section order for storytelling:
1. **Where** (geography/maps) → establishes scale
2. **Which shark** (species) → builds the villain cast
3. **When & what were you doing** (time, season, activity) → actionable insight
4. **Who** (demographics) → personal connection
5. **vs California** (comparison) → global context, second source

This order moves from broad to specific, and from descriptive to analytical — matching the Munzner What/Why/How framework your tutor is looking for.
