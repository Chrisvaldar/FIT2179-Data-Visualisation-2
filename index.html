Sharks in
Australian Waters

Over 230 years and 1,196 recorded incidents, the story of sharks and Australians is more nuanced than the headlines suggest. Who gets attacked, which sharks are truly dangerous, and does the risk actually keep growing?

Section 1

What does the shark attack history look like in Australia?

New South Wales bears the brunt, but no coast is safe
Outcome
Fatal Injured Uninjured

Sydney Harbour and the northern beaches carry the densest concentration of incidents on the map, with 438 incidents in New South Wales alone. A second cluster runs up the Gold Coast and Byron Bay corridor. Fatal incidents are not locked to any famous beach: red dots are scattered from Tasmania to the Top End, with no single stretch of coastline dominating the death toll.

Use the decade brush on the timeline below to filter the map by era. Drag to the 1800s and you get a thin scatter of red and orange dots almost entirely around Sydney Harbour. Drag to the 2000s onwards and the map fills in along the entire east coast, with WA appearing far more prominently too.

The 2010s recorded 231 incidents, nearly double the 164 from the 2000s, and on raw count alone that looks alarming. But notice how the colour composition of the stack changes over time. Early decades are heavily red and orange, meaning almost every incident was fatal or seriously injuring.

By the 2010s, the grey uninjured band has grown substantially, partly because minor encounters that would never have been logged in 1920 are now reported. Switch to per million people and the crisis disappears entirely. Since Federation in 1901 the per capita rate has stayed nearly flat even as raw counts climbed fivefold. The modern surge is a story of more Australians in the water, not more aggressive sharks.

More attacks does not mean more deaths

Northern Territory tops the per capita rate at 75.9 incidents per million people, with Western Australia close behind at 70.6 per million along a coastline that stretches over 12,000 km. Victoria sits at the bottom at around 10.5 per million, consistent with colder water and far less year-round beach activity than the northern states.

New South Wales leads with 438 incidents but sits mid-pack on fatality rate. South Australia and Queensland share the highest fatality rate at around 27%, a figure partly explained by the dominance of large white sharks in South Australia’s cooler southern waters around the Neptune Islands and Eyre Peninsula, where great whites congregate year-round.

Although New South Wales dominates in raw incident count, a bite in South Australia or Queensland is far more likely to be fatal, with fatality rates sitting at 27% compared to New South Wales’s 18%. So if you ever find yourself in a shark incident, hope it is not off the coast of South Australia.

Section 2

Which shark should you actually fear?

Three sharks bite a lot and kill. One just bites a lot.

White, tiger, and bull sharks account for nearly all 250 recorded fatalities across the dataset. White sharks lead with 91 deaths, tiger sharks sit at 86, and bull sharks at 63.

Click any of these three species in the Sankey and watch where their bands go: the flow to fatal is thick and unmistakable. These three share one thing beyond size. They are all comfortable hunting in the same shallow coastal water where Australians swim, surf, and snorkel.

Now click wobbegong. With 201 incidents it rivals white sharks in bite count, yet its band flows almost entirely into injured and barely touches fatal. That is zero deaths across 201 incidents.

Wobbegongs are bottom-dwelling ambush predators that bite when you step on them or pick them up, then immediately let go. They are not hunting you. They are just annoyed. So if raw bite count does not tell you how dangerous a shark is, maybe size does?

The smallest guy likes to hurt people.

The wobbegong sits at 1.2 m median, the smallest species here, yet its bite count rivals the deadliest sharks in the database. Size and bite count simply do not correlate. At 3.0 m median, white and tiger sharks are more than twice the length, and that difference is not cosmetic. A shark above 2 m can generate enough bite force to reach vital organs in a single motion. A wobbegong physically cannot. Size sets the ceiling on damage, which is why the two measurements that matter most both belong to the two deadliest species.

The other shark worth watching is the bull shark. It does not top any single category but it is the one species comfortable in rivers, estuaries, and harbour inlets as well as open ocean. The Brisbane River, Parramatta River, and Swan River all have bull shark records going back to the 1800s. People do not expect a 2.4 m predator in a river swimming hole, which is part of what makes bull sharks disproportionately dangerous.

Section 3

Does when and how you swim change your odds?

Swimmers die the most. Surfers mostly walk away.

Swimming is the only activity that dominates both sides of this chart at once, with 451 incidents on the left and a 34.6% fatality rate on the right, well above the overall average of 22.6%. That double dominance is unusual. High-frequency activities normally carry lower fatality rates because minor incidents drag the average down. Swimming does not follow that pattern.

A swimmer is fully submerged and slow-moving, presenting a silhouette from below that a large shark can easily mistake for prey, with nothing between the teeth and the body.

Surfing and boarding generate 284 incidents but only a 9.5% fatality rate, largely because most bites are investigatory. The shark makes contact once and retreats, and the board itself puts physical material between the teeth and the torso.

Spearfishing sits in an interesting middle position: the shark is reacting to a speared fish, bites defensively once, and leaves, keeping fatality low despite the violence. Fishing and boating sit at zero deaths across 70 incidents because the victim is rarely fully submerged. If you want to cut both your encounter rate and your fatality odds, stay on the board.

The clock is almost empty between midnight and 5 am, just 11 incidents across those five hours. Incidents build through the morning, with a bump at 11am, then surge to a sharp peak at 4 pm with 55 total incidents.

That 4pm number lines up directly with peak beach attendance during Australian summers. More people in the water means more encounters, but the low afternoon sun angle also creates surface glare that cuts underwater visibility in both directions, driving more investigatory bites from sharks that cannot clearly identify what they are approaching.

Use the dropdown to isolate fatal incidents only and the inner ring tracks the outer ring almost exactly. fatal share stays roughly constant across every hour of the day. Time of day changes how likely you are to encounter a shark, but once that encounter happens, the clock does not change your odds of surviving it. That comes down to something else entirely: who you are.

Who is most at risk?

Nine in ten victims are male.

Males account for 1,060 of 1,177 victims, roughly 90% of all recorded incidents. The male fatality rate sits at 21.9% compared to 13.7% for females. This is not a biology story, it is an exposure story. Men dominate the activities that put you in prolonged contact with deep water: surfing, spearfishing, diving, and board sports, while women are more likely to be swimming in shallower, patrolled water closer to shore.

A man spearfishing at 10 metres and a woman swimming inside the flags at Bondi face completely different risk profiles, and the aggregate numbers simply reflect that gap. The fatality rate difference (21.9% vs 13.7%) is real but smaller than the volume gap, which tells you the gap is almost entirely about who is in the water and how deep. Gender tells us about exposure. But there is another dimension that cuts across both: age.

If you are young and in the water, you are the most at risk.

Age is recorded for 697 of 1,203 incidents. The fatal peak sits at age 16 with 14 incidents, and most deaths cluster tightly in the 10 to 25 range. But notice how the fatal ridge spreads further into the 20s and 30s than the survived curve. Older victims are more likely to be in solo, deeper-water activities like diving and spearfishing, far from shore and from help, which pushes survival odds down even when the encounter itself is no more violent.

Among those who survived, the peak sits at age 17 with 27 incidents, just one year older than the fatal peak. Both curves occupy the same age range because teenagers and young adults simply spend the most time in the water. Survived incidents outnumber fatal ones at almost every age under 30, but that advantage narrows as age increases. Being young does not protect you. It just means there are more of you in the water, taking more risks, in more places.

Section 5

How does Australia compare with California?

New South Wales and California: same surf culture, completely different coastlines.
Outcome
Fatal Injured Uninjured

New South Wales recorded 286 incidents since 1950, making it the most direct peer for California’s 202 white shark incidents across the same period. NSW dots spread across hundreds of kilometres of coastline, clustering around Sydney and the Central Coast with a second band near Byron Bay. Use the decade filter to step through both maps together and watch California’s map go from nearly empty in the 1970s to rapidly filling from the 1990s onwards, tracking white shark population recovery after federal protection laws in 1994.

California tells a far tighter geographic story. Roughly seven in ten incidents fall inside the corridor from San Diego to Mendocino, the stretch surfers call the Red Triangle, where cold upwellings support large seal populations and the white sharks that follow them. Both regions are board-dominated, with 51% of NSW incidents involving surfing or boarding against 43% in California. The difference is not culture or risk appetite. It is coastline geometry.

New South Wales injures at 8x the rate of California. The death share is almost identical.
Region
California New South Wales

Adjusted for population, NSW dominates the injured category at 22.78 incidents per million against California’s 2.75, a gap of roughly 8 times. Fatalities stay much closer, with NSW at 2.45 per million and California at 0.38. The injured gap reflects both the diversity of shark species in Australian waters and the sheer volume of people entering a warm, year-round coastline.

Switch to raw incident count and one number stands out: uninjured totals are nearly identical, at 80 for NSW and 79 for California, despite the massive difference in coastline length and population exposure. After 230 years, 250 fatalities, and 1,196 recorded encounters, the clearest conclusion is that shark risk in Australia is real but almost entirely shaped by what you do in the water, not some inevitable feature of where you swim.
