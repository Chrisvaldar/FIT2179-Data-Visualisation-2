Sharks in
Australian Waters

Over 230 years and 1,196 recorded incidents, the story of sharks and Australians is more nuanced than the headlines suggest. Who gets attacked, which sharks are truly dangerous, and does the risk actually keep growing?

Section 1

What does the shark attack history look like in Australia?

NSW bears the brunt, but no coast is safe

Sydney Harbour and the northern beaches carry the densest concentration of incidents on the map, with a second cluster running up the Gold Coast and Byron Bay corridor. The surge in dots after 2000 reflects population growth and better reporting as much as any change in shark behaviour.

Fatal incidents are not locked to any famous beach. They are scattered across the entire coastline, from Tasmania to the Top End.

More attacks does not mean more deaths

NT tops the per capita rate at 75.9 incidents per million people, with WA close behind at 70.6 per million along a coastline that stretches over 12,000 km. Victoria sits at the bottom at around 10.5 per million, consistent with colder water and far less year-round beach activity than the northern states.

NSW leads with 438 incidents but sits mid-pack on fatality rate. SA and QLD share the highest fatality rate at around 27%, a figure partly explained by the dominance of large white sharks in SA’s cooler southern waters around the Neptune Islands and Eyre Peninsula, where great whites congregate year-round. Although NSW dominates in raw incident count, a bite in SA or QLD is far more likely to be fatal, with fatality rates sitting at 27% compared to NSW’s 18%. So if you ever find yourself in a shark incident, hope it is not off the coast of South Australia.

The 2010s recorded 231 incidents, nearly double the 164 from the 2000s, and on raw count alone that looks like a crisis. Switch to incidents per million people and the chart collapses. The 1790s sit at around 200 per million, a figure that reflects how few people were in Australia at the time rather than any genuine explosion of shark activity. Since Federation in 1901, the per capita rate has stayed remarkably flat, hovering in low single digits even as raw counts climbed.

The WW2 dip is visible in the raw count view, consistent with fewer civilians on beaches during wartime. The modern surge is almost entirely a story of more Australians in the water, not more aggressive sharks.

Section 2

Which shark should you actually fear?

Three sharks bite a lot and kill. One just bites a lot.

White, tiger, and bull sharks account for nearly all 250 recorded fatalities, with white sharks leading at 91 deaths, tiger sharks at 86, and bull sharks at 63. These three species share one trait: they are large, genuinely predatory, and comfortable in the same shallow coastal water where Australians swim.

The wobbegong tells the opposite story. With 201 incidents it ranks second only to white sharks in bite count, yet its flow terminates almost entirely in the injured column. Zero deaths across 201 incidents. Wobbegongs are bottom-dwelling ambush predators that bite when stepped on or handled, then let go. So if bite count does not tell you how dangerous a shark is, does size?

The smallest guy likes to hurt people.

The wobbegong sits at the top with 201 incidents and a median length of just 1.2 m, the smallest of every species in this chart. Size and bite count simply do not correlate.

Where size does matter is at the deadly end: white sharks at 3.0 m median and 361 incidents, and tiger sharks at 3.0 m with 229 incidents, are both among the largest and the most fatal species in the database. For the sharks that actually kill, size and danger align. For the wobbegong, the only thing it has going for it is patience.

Section 3

Does when and how you swim change your odds?

Swimmers die the most. Surfers mostly walk away.

Swimming dominates both sides of this chart: 451 incidents and a 34.6% fatality rate, far above the overall average of 22.6%. The gap comes down to body position. A swimmer is fully submerged, moving slowly, and presents a silhouette from below that is uncomfortably similar to a seal.

Surfing and boarding generate 284 incidents but only a 9.5% fatality rate, largely because shark bites in this context tend to be investigatory rather than predatory. The shark bites once and retreats. Fishing and boating together account for 70 incidents and zero deaths, which makes sense given the victim is rarely fully in the water. If you want to minimise both the chance of an incident and the chance of dying from one, stay on the board.

Peak hour is 4pm, not dawn.

The dial is nearly empty between midnight and 5 am, just 11 incidents across those five hours. Incidents build through the morning, spike at 11 am with 45, then peak sharply at 4 pm with 55 total and 17 fatal.

The 4 pm peak aligns with peak beach attendance during Australian summers and with the low afternoon sun angle that creates glare and reduces underwater visibility, making it harder for swimmers to spot approaching sharks and harder for sharks to identify what they are approaching. Crucially, the fatal inner ring tracks the outer ring without spiking at any specific hour. The time of day changes how likely you are to encounter a shark. It does not change your odds of surviving if you do.

Section 4

Who is most at risk?

Nine in ten victims are male.

Males account for 1,060 of 1,177 victims, roughly 90% of all recorded incidents. The male fatality rate sits at 21.9% compared to 13.7% for females. This is not a biology story, it is an exposure story. Men consistently spend more time in the surf, swim further from shore, and dominate activities like spearfishing, diving, and board sports that involve prolonged submersion in deeper water.

A man spearfishing in 10 metres of water and a woman swimming inside the flags at Bondi face completely different risk profiles. The aggregate numbers simply reflect that difference.

If you are young and in the water, you are the most at risk.

Age is only recorded for 697 of 1,203 incidents, but the pattern is clear. Among fatal attacks, the sharpest concentration sits at age 16 with 14 incidents, and most deaths fall between 10 and 25. The fatal ridge spreads further into the 20s and 30s than the survived curve, but it never leaves the young adult range.

Among those who survived, the single year peak is age 17 with 27 incidents, the same broad 10 to 25 cluster. Both outcomes skew young because teenagers and young adults spend the most time in the water. Survived incidents outnumber fatal ones at almost every age under 30. Being young does not protect you; it just means there are more of you in the water.

Section 5

How does Australia compare with California?

NSW and California: same surf culture, completely different coastlines.
Outcome
Fatal Injured Uninjured

New South Wales is Australia’s shark incident capital with 286 incidents since 1950, making it the most direct comparison to California’s 202 white shark incidents in the same period. NSW incidents cluster around Sydney and the Central Coast with a second band around Byron Bay, spread across hundreds of kilometres of coastline.

California tells a tighter story. Roughly seven in ten incidents fall inside the corridor from San Diego to Mendocino, a stretch surfers call the Red Triangle. Both states are dominated by board culture, with 51% of NSW incidents involving surfing or boarding against 43% in California. The difference is not appetite for risk. It is geography.

NSW injures at 8x the rate of California. The death share is almost identical.
Region
California NSW

Adjusted for population, NSW dominates the injured category at 22.78 incidents per million against California’s 2.75, a gap of roughly 8 times. Fatalities stay much closer, with NSW at 2.45 per million and California at 0.38. The per capita view puts both states on a level footing and makes the injured gap impossible to ignore.

Switch to raw incident count and the pattern holds. NSW leads on injured (186 vs 108), while uninjured totals are almost tied (80 vs 79). NSW has already been normalised for population, so the gap is not just because more people live there. Australians simply encounter sharks more often, and the numbers reflect that regardless of how you look at them.
