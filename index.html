Sharks in

Over 230 years and 1,196 recorded incidents, the story of sharks and Australians is more nuanced than the headlines suggest. Who gets attacked, which sharks are truly dangerous, and does the risk actually keep growing?

Section 1

Setting the Scene: Australia and Sharks

Every dot is a real incident. The coastal concentration along New South Wales and Queensland is immediate, but the choropleth tells a different story when you switch from raw counts to fatality rates.

Figure 1.1 — Incident locations, 1791–2022. Use the decade filter to watch incidents cluster. Pre-1900 incidents are concentrated around Sydney Harbour, reflecting colonial-era swimming culture. The modern spread reflects both population growth and improved reporting.

Figure 1.2 — Incidents and fatality rates by state. Toggle between metrics. NSW accounts for 438 incidents but its fatality rate is lower than Queensland or South Australia. Western Australia has a fearsome reputation, but falls mid-range on both measures.

Figure 1.3 — Incidents per decade, raw and population-adjusted. The post-2000 surge in raw counts is real, but the rate per million Australians peaked in the 1930s and has been broadly flat since the 1960s. More people at the beach, not more dangerous water.

Section 2

Which Shark Should You Actually Fear?

The most common shark in the dataset is not the most dangerous. The species breakdown subverts the Jaws narrative.

Figure 2.1 — Species by incident count, fatality rate, and size. Bubble area encodes median shark length. Colour encodes what proportion of that species' attacks were provoked. The wobbegong cluster sits bottom-left: 201 incidents, 0% fatal, small and defensive. Tiger sharks sit top-right.

Figure 2.2 — Wobbegong incidents by activity. Every single wobbegong bite is non-fatal. The activity breakdown confirms why: almost all occur while the victim is diving, snorkelling, or standing in water. The shark bites when disturbed, not when hunting.

Section 3

Where, When, and What Were You Doing?

Risk is not evenly distributed. The charts below profile the circumstances of attacks to give an actionable picture of who gets bitten and why.

Figure 3.1 — Activity risk: volume vs fatality rate. Swimming leads on both axes. Surfing and boarding generate many incidents but a low fatality rate, consistent with defensive bites. Fishing and boating record zero deaths across all incidents.
